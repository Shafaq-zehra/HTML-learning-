LABEL OF DYSLEXIA AND ITS PYSCHLOGICAL IMPACTS

AUTHOR : SHAFAQ ZEHRA
student at FCCU

Just imagine, a child barely eight or nine years old, can’t read or write properly, can’t do a lot of things that other children of his age do effortlessly; what must he be going through? His self-confidence must have shattered. One of the most often overlooked aspects of coming to terms with all aspects of dyslexia throughout one's life is the emotional side. Usually, teachers and parents are good at identifying academic difficulties in their students. But they can overlook the growing lack of motivation, low self-esteem, and depression that develops as the child progresses through school and the stress increase. This has long-term consequences because dyslexia is a condition that does not go away. This paper analyzes the emotional causes and effects of ignoring the early identification of dyslexia. The late diagnosis can result in frustration, pressure, and less self-belief.

Moreover, continuous failure can cause loss of self-confidence and inferiority in dyslexic children; they begin to feel that their efforts are inadequate. The parents and teachers fail to understand that while treating such students, their care, love, and support can change the life of a dyslexic student. The label of dyslexia prohibits teachers and parents from helping children and adults learn individually, resulting in failure to perform and depression.

HISTORY OF DYSLEXIA

Despite its relatively recent rise to prominence, dyslexia was first recognized in the late 1800s. In 1878, Adolph Kussmaul, a German neurologist, was the first to discover reading disabilities in people. He was particularly interested in people who had reading difficulties as well as neurological disabilities. Rudolf Berlin, a German ophthalmologist, was the first to adopt the term "dyslexia" instead of "word blindness" in 1887. Pringle-Morgan, a general practitioner, published the first case of developmental dyslexia in the British Medical Journal on November 7, 1896. Dr. Samuel T. Orton, an American neurologist, proposed how particular reading difficulties emerged in 1925. He put much attention on one side of the brain's superiority. He was one of the first scholars to explain dyslexia's emotional dimensions. The majority of dyslexic preschoolers, according to his study, are satisfied and well adjusted. When early reading practice does not fit their learning style, emotional difficulties emerge and cause depression and failure to perform.. His study supports the point that constant failure in the early years of education can lead to emotional problems.

Different kinds of particular learning disabilities were researched during this period. Still, it was not until 1939 that Dr. Alfred Struss and R. Heinz Werner published their results on children with a wide variety of learning difficulties that they became widely recognized. Their research highlighted the diversity of these issues and the importance of identifying each child's educational needs individually. His research supports the point that in schools each student should be paid attention and the dyslexic students need special attention and care.

CURRENT STATUS

Today,recent research shows that in the development of mental issues in a dyslexic child, the parents play an essential part. Some parents are saddened when they learn that their child has dyslexia, and they believe they have no choice but to lower their expectations for their child's future. Whereas, Some parents want to support their kids but do not figure out how to help them . After researching parent’s roles, it is undoubtedly apparent that if parents talk to their dyslexic children about their problems it can result in a positive approach.
